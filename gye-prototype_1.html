Module 01

Before they start

The work you do before day one determines how day one feels. Most of it takes less than an hour.

Checklist
0 / 6

Tell them what to expect

Young people — especially those who are neurodivergent or starting their first role — often arrive anxious about things experienced workers take for granted. Where do I go? What do I wear? Who do I ask for?

"I need people to be black and white with the information they give me."

— Young person, Kai & Kōrero session
  • Tell them what time to arrive and exactly where to go
  • Let them know what to wear — or that it genuinely doesn't matter
  • Tell them who to ask for when they arrive
  • Give them a heads-up on what the first day will roughly look like
  • Tell them they won't be expected to know everything immediately
  • Prepare your team — ask them to make the effort to welcome someone new

Make the implicit explicit

Every workplace has unwritten rules. For young people without work experience — or from different cultural backgrounds — these invisible rules are the biggest source of early confusion and exits.

Worth writing down
How do breaks actually work here? Is it okay to ask the same question twice? What does "on time" mean — exactly on the dot, or five minutes before?
What the research found
Young people said job descriptions often don't match reality. Being upfront about what the role actually involves builds trust from day one.

Module 02

First day

The first day sets the tone for everything that follows. It doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be human.

Checklist
0 / 5

The welcome matters more than the paperwork

It's tempting to start with forms and inductions. But young people consistently said the thing they remember is whether they felt welcomed as a person — not processed as a new hire.

"Welcome new employees with a mihi — informal or formal. Have food prepped and encourage interaction. Don't go straight into business."

— Youth voice survey participant
  • Introduce them to everyone by name — not just job title
  • Have somewhere clearly theirs — a desk, a locker, a hook for their bag
  • Assign a buddy — someone they can ask "silly" questions of without embarrassment
  • Share kai together if you can — even just morning tea
  • Don't start them on the busiest shift of the week

Silence isn't disengagement

Don't assume a young person not asking questions means they understand everything. Young people from Pasifika backgrounds may be hesitant to speak up. Neurodivergent employees process information differently. Build in a moment at the end of the day to check in privately — make it easy to say "I'm not sure about something."

"Asking questions may seem like a challenge or reflect badly. Younger Pasifika siblings do not speak around elders — and can slip into this mindset at work."

— Working group participant

Module 03

First week

The first week is about trust — both ways. Enough structure to feel safe, enough space to actually learn.

Checklist
0 / 5

Check in — don't check up

There's a real difference between checking in (how are you going, what do you need?) and checking up (are you doing what I told you?). Young people feel the difference immediately. A five-minute daily check-in in the first week makes a bigger difference than a formal review at the end of the month.

  • Schedule a 5-minute check-in each day for the first week
  • Ask "what's one thing that wasn't clear today?" and normalise it
  • Tell them explicitly: no question is a stupid question here
  • Give them a task they can complete successfully this week
  • Acknowledge when they do something well — out loud, specifically

What good looks like

The most common early exits come from mismatched expectations. Young people need to know what good looks like before they can hit the mark.

Be specific
"Here's what done looks like, and here's when I need it by" — not just "do a good job on this."
Deadlines work
For neurodivergent employees especially — clear deadlines help, not hinder. "If there's no deadline, why would I do it?"
Mistakes are part of it
Cool down, then address it. Making people feel like bad people reduces their willingness to try new things.

Module 04 — Young person profile

Know your person

Not a form to file away. A starting point for a conversation — fill it in together if you can.

Why this matters

The research is clear: young people want to be seen as individuals — not as "a young person" filling a role. The quickest way to build trust is to know something real about who they are before things go wrong.

"Ask the question: what do you need? Then actually listen to the answer."

— Youth voice survey participant

A note on disclosure

Young people told us they want to share what they need — but on their own terms, and only to the extent it affects their work. They shouldn't have to name a disability to receive basic flexibility.

What works
Ask "what would make this job easier for you?" early — before anything has gone wrong. This opens the door without requiring disclosure.
What doesn't
Requiring a young person to justify a disability to receive an accommodation. They may not know themselves yet what they need — it takes time and trial.
