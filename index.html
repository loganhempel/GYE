Module 01 · Before they start

The work you do before day one determines how day one feels.

Most of it takes under an hour. All of it matters.

Progress
0 of 7 done
"I need people to be black and white with the information they give me. Guessing makes me nervous."
Young person — Kai & Kōrero session, YEA 2025
01

Send a message before they arrive

Young people — especially those who are neurodivergent or starting their first role — often arrive anxious about things experienced workers take for granted. A simple text the day before removes most of that anxiety before it starts.

📍
Where to go and when
Give them the exact address, which entrance, what time to arrive, and who to ask for. Don't assume they'll figure it out — they won't ask.
Logistics
👕
What to wear
Be explicit — even if the answer is "whatever you're comfortable in." The absence of guidance creates anxiety, not freedom.
Logistics
🗓️
A rough shape of the day
Tell them what's roughly planned. Not every detail — just enough that they know what to expect when they walk in. Surprises feel threatening before trust is built.
Expectations
💬
Permission to not know everything
Say it out loud: "You won't be expected to know anything on your first day. We'll show you." This one line changes how they walk in the door.
Psychological safety
02

Write down your unwritten rules

Every workplace has rules nobody ever says out loud. For young people without experience — or who come from different cultural backgrounds — these invisible rules are the most common cause of early exits.

Breaks
Do people actually take them? Is it okay to leave your desk? Does 30 mins mean exactly 30?
Questions
Is it okay to ask the same question twice? Who's the right person to ask? When's a bad time to interrupt?
Mistakes
What happens when something goes wrong? Will they get in trouble for asking for help too early?
"On time"
Does on time mean exactly on the dot, five minutes before, or just before the work starts?
📋
Write a "this is how we work" note
One page. Plain language. Cover breaks, communication, expectations around questions, what happens when something goes wrong, and anything else that seems obvious to you but won't be obvious to them. Hand it over on day one.
High impact
👥
Brief your existing team
Tell them someone new is starting. Ask them specifically to introduce themselves, include the new person at break time, and be patient with questions. This doesn't happen naturally — ask for it.
Culture
Te ao Māori lens

Consider a pōwhiri-inspired welcome — where the team introduces themselves, their purpose, and their culture before the new person is expected to perform. Even informally, this sets a relational foundation before a transactional one.

Pasifika context

Younger siblings in many Pasifika families do not speak freely around elders. This deference to authority can look like silence or agreement in a workplace. Being explicit that questions are welcome — in private, without judgment — changes everything.

Neurodivergent context

Neurodivergent employees often arrive with significant anxiety about sensory environments, unspoken expectations, and social dynamics they can't read. A written "how we work" document is not just helpful — for many, it's essential to functioning well from day one.

🎯
Assign a buddy before they arrive
Choose someone on your team who is patient, approachable, and close enough in seniority that questions won't feel embarrassing. Tell the new person who it is before day one. Young people consistently said this was the single most important thing in their first weeks — having one specific person they knew they could ask.
Highest impact

How to welcome them in a way they'll actually remember.

Module 02 · First day

It doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be human.

The first day sets the tone for everything that follows. Young people remember how it felt — not what was on the agenda.

Progress
0 of 6 done
"Welcome new employees with a mihi — informal or formal. Have food prepped and encourage interaction. Don't go straight into doing business."
Youth voice survey participant — YEA 2025
01

The welcome matters more than the paperwork

Almost every young person in the research said the same thing: what they remember about their first day is whether they felt welcomed as a person — or processed like a task to be completed. The forms can wait. The welcome cannot.

👋
Introduce them as a person, not a title
Walk them around. Use names. Tell the team one interesting thing about them. "This is Alex, they're joining us in operations and they're obsessed with surfing" lands completely differently to "this is our new admin."
Belonging
🍵
Share kai — even just morning tea
Eating together is whanaungatanga in practice. It signals that this is a team, not just a workplace. It also gives them a reason to talk to people before the pressure of actual work starts.
Culture · High impact
📍
Give them a space that is clearly theirs
A desk. A locker. A hook for their bag. Somewhere to put their stuff. This sounds small but it signals permanence — they're not a visitor, they belong here.
Belonging
📅
Don't start them on the busiest day
Starting someone on your worst day guarantees they leave thinking that's every day. Start them on a slower day or quieter shift so they can actually absorb information and talk to people without everyone being too rushed to care.
Timing
02

Silence is not disengagement

Young people consistently said they were afraid to ask questions — not because they didn't have them, but because they didn't know if it was safe to ask. This is especially true for Pasifika and neurodivergent employees. Build the check-in into the day rather than waiting for them to come to you.

What they're thinking
"Will I look stupid if I ask?" · "Maybe everyone else already knows this." · "I'll figure it out on my own."
What actually helps
Ask the question yourself: "What's one thing that wasn't clear today?" Make it normal to have things unclear. Normalise it on day one.
The Pasifika reality
Younger siblings in many families do not speak freely around elders or authority. This respect can look like silence. It isn't disengagement — it's deference.
🔁
End-of-day 5-minute check-in
Book it in. Before they leave, pull them aside for five minutes. Ask: "What's one thing that went well? What's one thing that wasn't totally clear?" Write it down. Do it every day in week one. The questions stop feeling weird after day two.
Highest impact
🙋
Say "no question is stupid" — out loud
Don't assume they know this. Say it directly. "We'd rather you ask than guess. Nothing you ask will annoy us." Then prove it — when they do ask, respond warmly. Your reaction to the first question determines whether there's a second.
Psychological safety
Māori context

Rangatahi Māori may arrive knowing exactly what they want and what they won't compromise on — including having their values and tikanga respected. Don't assume a quiet start means low expectations. Often it's the opposite.

Neurodivergent context

First days are often overwhelming for neurodivergent employees — new sensory environment, new social dynamics, new unwritten rules all at once. Reduce the information load on day one. Give them a written summary of what was covered rather than expecting them to retain it all verbally.

Disabled employees

Ask early: "Is there anything about this environment or how we work that would be helpful to adjust?" Ask before anything goes wrong — not after. This removes the burden of disclosure without requiring them to explain or justify anything.

Building trust while they find their feet.

Module 03 · First week

Trust is built in small moments — not performance reviews.

The first week is about giving them enough structure to feel safe and enough space to actually learn.

Progress
0 of 6 done
"If you finish something and get praised for it, it makes you want to do more. That's all it takes. Just noticing."
Youth voice survey participant — YEA 2025
01

Check in — don't check up

There's a real difference between checking in (how are you going, what do you need?) and checking up (are you doing what I told you?). Young people feel the difference immediately. A five-minute daily check-in in the first week does more for trust than a formal review at month's end.

⏱️
Daily 5-min check-in, every day this week
Put it in your calendar. Ask two questions: "What's going well?" and "What's one thing that wasn't totally clear?" That's it. The structure of the question matters — open questions get real answers, closed ones get "fine."
Highest impact
🎯
Give them a task they can win
In week one, give them at least one task they can complete successfully and get genuine feedback on. Early wins build confidence. Confidence builds engagement. Don't start with the hardest thing — start with something real enough to matter but achievable enough to succeed at.
Confidence
📣
Acknowledge what they do well — specifically
"Good job" means almost nothing. "The way you handled that customer — staying calm and asking what they actually needed — that was exactly right" means everything. Be specific. Name the thing. Do it in earshot of others when appropriate.
Recognition
📖
Introduce them to the "why" of the business
Young people need to understand why the work matters — not just what to do. Take 15 minutes this week to explain what the business does, who it serves, and why their role contributes to it. "You're doing this because of XYZ" changes how people show up.
Purpose
02

Make "good" visible before asking for it

The most common early exits come from mismatched expectations — usually because nobody told the young person what "good" looked like before holding them to it. Set the standard clearly, then give them time to meet it.

Instead of this
"Do a good job on this." · "Just use your initiative." · "You should know how to handle that."
Say this
"Here's what done looks like." · "Here's when I need it by." · "If you're not sure, ask me before you guess — that's not a weakness here."
On mistakes
Cool down before you address it. Making someone feel like a bad person reduces their willingness to try. Address the action — not the person.
📝
Write down what you expect — before the week starts
For every task you give them this week, write the expected output, the deadline, and who they can ask if they're stuck. Share it with them. This sounds like admin — it's actually the most respectful thing you can do for someone new.
Clarity
🤝
Book a week-two check-in now
Before the week ends, set a formal 15-minute one-on-one for the end of week two. Tell them it's coming and what you'll cover: how they're going, what support they need, and what they can expect from you going forward. Having it booked reduces anxiety.
Structure
Neurodivergent context

Deadlines are not punishment for neurodivergent employees — they're motivating structure. "If there's no deadline, why would I do it?" came up repeatedly in the research. Be concrete about when things are needed, not vague about "when you get a chance."

Pasifika context

Pacific youth often excel under pressure and have strong intrinsic drive — but need to feel the relationship is genuine before they'll show it. The first week is about building that relationship. Don't just measure output. Invest in the person.

Māori context

Rangatahi Māori often want to understand the "why" deeply before committing. Take time this week to explain the purpose behind tasks, not just the tasks themselves. Alignment with values creates loyalty that task completion never will.

Module 04 · Know your person

The fastest way to build trust is to actually know who you hired.

This isn't a form to file away. Use these questions across the first week — it's a conversation, not an interview.

"Ask the question: what do you need? Then actually listen to the answer. Don't just have a gap between what's being said and what's being done."
Youth voice survey participant — YEA 2025
Why

Why this conversation matters

Young people told us clearly: they want to be seen as individuals — not as "a young person" or a representative of their group. The research found that employers who took time to understand how their new employee actually works best saw fewer early exits, stronger performance, and better relationships. This conversation is what makes the difference.

01
Ask them: "How do you prefer to receive feedback?"
Young people consistently said feedback caught them off guard — especially when delivered in front of others or immediately after something went wrong. Knowing their preference means you can deliver it in a way they can actually hear and use.
Directly and straight away
In writing so I can process it first
In a private one-on-one
After I've had time to sit with it
Check back in — I'm still figuring this out
02
Ask them: "How do you prefer to ask questions?"
Many young people — particularly those who are Pasifika, neurodivergent, or in their first job — feel significant anxiety about "interrupting" or looking like they don't know something. Knowing how they'd prefer to ask means they'll actually ask, instead of guessing and getting it wrong.
Drop by and ask in person whenever
Message or text first so I'm not put on the spot
Save them for our scheduled check-in
I'll figure it out — I'll only ask if really stuck
03
Ask them: "Is there anything about how you work best I should know?"
This is the open door question — asked before anything goes wrong, without requiring disclosure of anything sensitive. Many neurodivergent employees, disabled employees, and those managing personal circumstances need flexibility but won't ask for it unless explicitly invited to. This question is the invitation.
I work best with clear deadlines and structure
I need quiet time to process — I can't work well in noise
I have some commitments that affect specific days/times
I process things better when I can write rather than just hear
Nothing comes to mind right now — I'll let you know
04
Ask them: "What would make your first month easier?"
This question does two things. It shows them you're thinking about their experience, not just their output. And it often surfaces practical things — a slightly different start time, a quieter space for breaks, a preference for written instructions — that cost you almost nothing but matter enormously to them.

⚠

A note on disclosure

Young people told us clearly: they want to share what they need on their own terms, and only to the extent it affects their work. The questions above are designed to open the door without requiring them to name anything they're not ready to name.

What works

"What would make this job easier for you?" — asked early, before anything has gone wrong. This removes the burden of disclosure. They can answer at whatever level they're comfortable with.

What doesn't work

Requiring a young person to justify or name a disability before receiving an accommodation. They may not know what they need yet — it takes time and trial. Don't make them earn basic flexibility.

The goal

"In an ideal world, you shouldn't have to disclose. When you get the job you should just be asked: what can I do to support you?" — Youth voice survey participant. That's exactly what this conversation is.
